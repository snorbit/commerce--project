Choosing the Right Plan for You

One of the biggest decisions you'll make when getting a mobile phone is whether to go with a fixed-term contract or a pre-paid plan. Here's a breakdown of the key differences, advantages, and disadvantages of each.

What's the Difference?

  • Pre-Paid Plans: You pay for your phone service upfront. You choose a certain amount of calls, texts, and data, and when it runs out, you'll need to 'recharge' or wait until the next month.
  • Fixed-Term Contracts (or Post-Paid Plans): You sign up for a longer period, usually 12 or 24 months. You get a set amount of inclusions each month and pay your bill at the end of the month. The cost of a new phone is often bundled into the monthly payment.

Advantages of Pre-Paid Plans

  • Lower Monthly Cost: Pre-paid plans are often cheaper because you only pay for what you need.
  • Flexibility: You're not locked into a long-term contract. You can switch providers or plans whenever you want without paying a termination fee.
  • No Credit Checks: Because you pay upfront, there's usually no need for a credit check, which can be helpful if you're just starting to build your credit history.
  • No Overage Fees: You can't accidentally run up a huge bill. If you use up your data or credit, your service simply stops until you recharge.

Disadvantages of Pre-Paid Plans

  • Upfront Handset Cost: If you want the latest smartphone, you'll usually have to buy it outright, which can be a big upfront expense. Contract plans often spread the cost of the phone over the life of the contract.
  • Family Plans Can Be Cheaper on Contract: For families with multiple phones, the discounts offered on contract family plans can sometimes make them more affordable per person.
  • Fewer International Options: While it's getting better, some pre-paid plans have limited options for international calls and roaming.
  • Potential for Slower Data: Some pre-paid providers, which use the networks of the major carriers, may have their data speeds 'throttled' (slowed down) during busy periods or after you've used a certain amount of data.
